Level One Advertising
Level One offers a moderate level of advertising implementation into the car's system. This includes the infotainment screen and the car's audio system. The displayed advertisements will never be in the driver's way of operating the vehicle safely. Additionally, as the level One offers low personalisation, only selected data will be shared with TOL and advertisers. This will result in less relevant advertising being shown. TOL will collect and share driving habits and most common routes; GPS information.

Level Two Advertising
Level Two offers more advertising within the system compared to Level One. It again uses both the infotainment system screen and the vehicle's audio system. Additionally, a small portion of the driver's gauge cluster is reserved for advertising purposes. As Level Two operates with higher personalisation, in addition to GPS data, TOL will utilise the vehicle's microphones to gauge topics of interest from the cabin. This function can be temporarily disabled in the car's settings, however it will turn on automatically after five minutes and on start. None of the advertising features will prevent the driver from safe operation of the vehicle.

The long print


The trickiest mode advertising must deal with. Car infotainments are already often criticised for moving most – if not all – of their controls to the touch screen, forcing the drive to take their eyes off the road (Kessels, 2024). Understandably, introducing distracting advertisements into the mix would likely cause public and legislative outrage. To mitigate this, the advertising in the proposed systems never seeks more attention that other tasks on the screen. Visual advertisements have a set place within the home screen and never overlay other apps during driving. The driver can interact with them only to the degree any other control would require them, never requiring more than one or two taps of the screen with minimal text. Alongside visual advertising – and as a part of it – is the audio advertising. Since the media is playing through the car’s system, the advertisement will not start mid-song or a phone call and will scan for a suitable gap in a podcast before starting. If the advertisement is geographically sensitive it will give the option to reroute to the address of the business or be saved, using buttons on the steering wheel. All advertising is ‘skippable’ after a certain time. No advertising will start during a parking sequence.

The long print


Often forgotten, however it is the most common state of the vehicle pointed out by Kessel (2024). The vehicle is parked, and the driver is not in it. This may seem like the car’s infotainment has no reach, yet that is not the case. As important as the in-car system nowadays is the accompanying mobile application. Through the application many functions can be accessed and many of them make more sense on the phone more than within the car. The phone application is where most interaction with the advertising can happen. Whenever the driver receives an advertising message, they have the option to save the message. This message is then stored in their account and is sent in the form of a push notification to their phone once they have locked the vehicle. This notification includes the promotion from the advertisement but also allows them to visit the website or take different actions depending on the message – while it is safe, and they are not driving anymore.
